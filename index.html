Dancing

I also love dancing. Dancing is my passion. I learn two very different genres of dance, house and freestyle. House dance has roots in the underground house music scene of Chicago and New York. It is typically danced to loud and bass-heavy electronic dance music provided by DJs. Freestyle is a way of dancing in which the dancer improvises his/her moves on the spot, as he/she dances, instead of having them planned (i.e. choreography). In its most extreme form, it is the opposite of choreography; however, it can be argued that such an intense level can never be reached, as we all, to some degree, plan our moves. While dancing, I/you/he/she can do the gracefully or go hard. I like to dance gracefully when the song is slow and wants to go hard when it is fast and upbeat. Usually, when the music slows down, dancers go down on the floor and stand and dance while the song is active and lively.


My Inspiration

Galileo Galilei



Galileo Galilei is my inspiration. I’ve always wanted to become an astronomer when I grow up. Studying the stars, the universe, the planets, and other heavenly bodies, will be fabulous. When I was five, I stared at the sky and asked my father, ‘What are the stars made of? Why do they shine so brightly?’. I know the answer to all these questions now, but I still don’t know the answer to another billion questions! Let me tell you a few facts about Galileo Galilei:

1. Galileo has been called the “father of observational astronomy”, the “father of modern physics”, the “father of the scientific method”, and the “father of modern science”.

2. Galileo developed one of the first telescopes.

3. In early 1610, he made the first in an extraordinary series of discoveries. He spent numerous weeks examining a set of stars near Jupiter as they revolved around the planet. He had discovered Jupiter’s four moons, which he named the Medicean Stars, but which have since been renamed the Galilean moon, in his honour.

4.Galileo also observed the phases of planet Venus and the existence of far more stars in the Milky Way that weren’t visible to the naked eye.
